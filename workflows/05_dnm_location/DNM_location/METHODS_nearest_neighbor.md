# Supplemental Methods: Spatial-clustering (nearest-neighbour) permutation test for de novo mutations

## Overview

To test whether de novo mutations (DNMs) are non-randomly distributed along the
autosomes — for example clustered into mutational hotspots, or more evenly
spaced than expected — we compared the observed spatial spacing of DNMs against
a null distribution generated by randomly placing the same number of mutations
across the callable genome. The summary statistic is the average
nearest-neighbour distance (ANND) between DNMs on the same chromosome, and
significance is assessed by permutation. The test was applied independently to
all seven *Stegodyphus* species (three social: *S. dumicola* (DUM),
*S. mimosarum* (MIM), *S. sarasinorum* (SAR); four subsocial:
*S. tentoriicola* (TEN), *S. bicolor* (BIC), *S. africanus* (AFR),
*S. lineatus* (LIN)) using an identical, configuration-driven Grid Workflow
(`gwf`) pipeline run on a SLURM cluster; species names, sex-chromosome labels,
and all input paths are taken from per-species configuration files rather than
hard-coded.

## De novo mutation set

The analysis was restricted to autosomes: sex chromosomes, listed per species
in the configuration (`x_chromosomes`, e.g. `afr_X1`, `afr_X2`), were removed
before any computation. The remaining DNM calls were deduplicated to unique
mutational events on the key (chromosome, position, reference allele, alternate
allele, father, mother), so that a mutation transmitted to, and therefore
called in, several siblings of the same family is counted once rather than once
per offspring. The number of unique autosomal DNM events retained per species
defines the sample size *N* used in the test (34–239 events; see Results).

## Callable-genome null space

The null model places mutations only where a mutation could actually have been
detected, i.e. within the callable genome. For each offspring, the per-base
callable-site records were merged into BED segments by joining consecutive
callable positions and bridging intra-segment gaps of at most 100 bp
(`merge_gap = 100`), using standard 0-based half-open coordinates. For each
species, the callable BED segments of all offspring were then pooled and reduced
to a per-chromosome union of overlapping or adjacent segments. This union — the
set of genomic positions callable in at least one offspring — constitutes the
sampling space for the permutations and spans approximately 1.4–2.0 Gb per
species.

## Test statistic: average nearest-neighbour distance

All unique autosomal DNM events of a species — pooled across every individual
and family — were combined into a single per-chromosome set of positions before
distances were computed; the test therefore evaluates the spatial arrangement of
DNMs at the level of the whole species cohort rather than within single
individuals, and a given mutation's nearest neighbour may originate from a
different individual. For a given set of mutation positions, the
nearest-neighbour distance of each mutation is the distance (in bp) to the
closest other mutation located on the same chromosome. The average
nearest-neighbour distance (ANND) is the mean of these minimum distances across
all mutations. A mutation that is the only event
on its chromosome has no within-chromosome neighbour and contributes no value to
the average; such single-mutation chromosomes are therefore excluded. The
identical statistic and the identical exclusion rule were applied to the
observed DNMs and to every permutation, so the two are directly comparable. A
smaller ANND than expected indicates spatial clustering, and a larger ANND than
expected indicates over-dispersion (more even spacing than chance).

## Permutation procedure

The null distribution of the ANND was generated by repeatedly drawing *N*
positions (equal to the number of unique autosomal DNM events) at random from
the callable-genome union. Sampling was uniform over callable base pairs: each
union segment was selected with probability proportional to its length, and a
position was then drawn uniformly within the selected segment, so that every
callable base had equal probability of being chosen. For each draw the ANND was
recomputed exactly as for the observed data (including the single-mutation
chromosome exclusion). This procedure was repeated for 1,000 permutations per
species. Sampling used NumPy's `default_rng` pseudo-random generator initialised
with a fixed seed (42), making every permutation set fully reproducible.

## Significance assessment

The observed ANND was compared to the 1,000 permuted ANND values with two
one-tailed empirical p-values: the clustering p-value is the fraction of
permutations whose ANND is less than or equal to the observed value, and the
dispersion p-value is the fraction of permutations whose ANND is greater than or
equal to the observed value. The reported two-tailed p-value is twice the
smaller of these two one-tailed values. In addition, a standardized effect size
was computed as a Z-score, Z = (observed ANND − mean of permuted ANND) /
standard deviation of permuted ANND, where negative Z indicates clustering and
positive Z indicates over-dispersion relative to the null. For each species the
test produced a per-permutation ANND table, a plain-text summary of these
statistics, and a histogram of the null distribution with the observed value and
the permuted mean marked; a combined multi-panel figure arranges the seven
species by social system (social and subsocial columns).

## Results

Across all seven species the observed ANND fell well within the permuted null
distribution, and no species showed a significant departure from a uniform
distribution of DNMs across the callable genome (all two-tailed p > 0.05,
|Z| ≤ 1.3).

| Species | Social system | Unique autosomal DNMs | Observed ANND (Mb) | Permuted mean ANND (Mb) | Z | p (two-tailed) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| *S. dumicola* (DUM)      | social    | 89  | 15.40 | 16.25 | −0.50 | 0.622 |
| *S. mimosarum* (MIM)     | social    | 75  | 17.68 | 18.46 | −0.36 | 0.748 |
| *S. sarasinorum* (SAR)   | social    | 34  | 34.01 | 39.55 | −0.68 | 0.506 |
| *S. tentoriicola* (TEN)  | subsocial | 82  | 16.75 | 16.40 | +0.19 | 0.832 |
| *S. bicolor* (BIC)       | subsocial | 239 | 5.82  | 5.56  | +0.80 | 0.404 |
| *S. africanus* (AFR)     | subsocial | 123 | 12.15 | 10.98 | +1.26 | 0.198 |
| *S. lineatus* (LIN)      | subsocial | 178 | 6.61  | 6.77  | −0.33 | 0.736 |

The DNMs were therefore neither significantly clustered nor significantly
over-dispersed along the autosomes in any species: their spatial arrangement is
statistically indistinguishable from a random placement across the callable
genome, providing no evidence for localized mutational hotspots at this scale.

## Reproducibility

The entire test was implemented as a `gwf` workflow on a SLURM cluster, with the
analysis step (`nearest_neighbor_test.py`) and the cross-species plotting step
(`plot_nearest_neighbor_all_species.py`) pinned to a dedicated conda environment
(`python_phylo`; Python 3 with NumPy, pandas, and Matplotlib). Each species was
processed from an independent configuration file specifying its DNM calls,
callable-genome directory, sex-chromosome labels, and the number of permutations
(1,000) and random seed (42). All inputs, scripts, per-species summaries,
per-permutation tables, histograms, and the combined figure are available in the
project repository under `steps/nearest_neighbor_test/`.
